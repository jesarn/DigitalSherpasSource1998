(LOS ANGELES) - The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences has lined up the vocal power for its March 23 Oscar ceremony.

Singing nominated original songs will be Michael Bolton ("Go the Distance" from Hercules), Trisha Yearwood ("How Do I Live" from Con Air, which won a Grammy last week) and 19-year-old Aaliyah ("Journey to the Past" from Anastasia).

Elliott Smith, who wrote the music and lyric for "Miss Misery" from Good Will Hunting, will perform his song, and Celine Dion will sing "My Heart Will Go On" from Titanic.

(NEW YORK) - Grammy winners Celine Dion and Kenneth "Babyface" Edmonds will perform Oct. 23 at the Carousel of Hope, one of the world's largest one-night fundraisers.

Backing the Carousel's music lineup at the benefit, which will be held at the Beverly Hilton in Beverly Hills, Calif., will be producer/arranger/composers David Foster and Quincy Jones.

"The Tonight Show's" Jay Leno, a longtime supporter of the fight against childhood diabetes, will again serve as master of ceremonies. Merv Griffin, who owns the Beverly Hilton, will again appear as host. Now in its 20th year, the Carousel of Hope raises money to combat children's diabetes.

Proceeds from the event benefit the Barbara Davis Center for Childhood Diabetes -- a research and treatment center where more than 2,500 children receive care -- as well as the Los Angeles chapters of the American Diabetes Assn. and the Juvenile Diabetes Foundation International.

(NEW YORK) - Jon Bon Jovi, Toni Braxton, Hanson and the Backstreet Boys were among the 24 winners Thursday at the Echo Awards, the German equivalent of the Grammy Awards.

Among the most prominent local stars who went home with prizes were the heavy metal group Rammstein, BMG's hit girl group Tic Tac Toe (which has since regrouped as Tic Tac Two), hip-hop singers Nana and Sabrina Setlur and jazzman Klaus Doldinger.

The Comedian Harmonists, the German a cappella group popular in the 1930s and '40s, were given an award for life's work. The story of the group formed the basis for a popular local film that was recently picked up by Miramax. German actor Ben Becker, who portrayed one of the group's members in the recent Senator-produced was scheduled to accept the award on their behalf. Only one of the group's six singers is still living.

(NEW YORK) - Under the wing of new management -- her mom -- Jewel is forging ahead with a slew of projects, including her film debut.

The Atlantic artist has dropped manager Inga Vainshtein and will now be represented solely by her mother, Nedra Carroll. The San Diego-based Carroll has no prior management experience. She says the shooting of Jewel's first film may push back the release of her next album, planned for late October or early November. Jewel has not yet chosen a producer or begun recording the set.

The singer/songwriter is currently in Kansas shooting her leading role in the Civil War epic To Live On, directed by Ang Lee (The Ice Storm), which should open in December or early next year. Meanwhile, her HarperCollins poetry book is due at the end of May, and her "scrapbook" will likely come shortly after the album is released.

Aimee Osbourne, the teenaged daughter of rocker Ozzy Osbourne, was the highest bidder at Christie's City of Hope celebrity auction on Thursday evening for a day with the teen pop stars Hanson, a prize that commanded $16,000.

More than 250 bidders joined Ms. Osbourne at the standing-room only event at Christie's in Los Angeles. The Ozzman's daughter, who claims she really bought the concert tickets and backstage pass for her 13-year old sister, left the auction with one of the most expensive gifts of the evening.

One bidder paid $16,000 for a dinner for 10 prepared by Wolfgang Puck and another walked off with a $12,000 basketball duel with the L.A. Laker Shaquille O'Neal. Lunch with supermodel Cindy Crawford went for $7,500, while the most expensive prize of the night -- a week as editor of HITS magazine -- was purchased for $20,000 by Norman J. Pattiz, chairman of Westwood One Inc..

All in all, more than $131,000 was raised for City of Hope.

In the last 25 years, the City of Hope Music and Entertainment Industry has raised more than $28 million to benefit victims of cancer, diabetes, HIV/AIDS and other fatal diseases. This one-time event was made possible by artists who wished to support the City of Hope's crusade, and according to the head auctioneer at Christie's, helped raise more money than expected. (Lana Fanelli)
